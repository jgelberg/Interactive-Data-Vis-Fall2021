Defunct Social Media Websites

AIM 1997 AIM allowed for instant communication with friends and family via the "instant message" and famed "buddy list". At its peak, it had 53 million users in 2006. 2017
Club Penguin 2005 Club Penguin was an interactive social media site where users (kids and teens) could interact with others using their penguin avatar and play games in this virtual world. In 2007, it was sold to Disney for an estimated amount of 700 million dollars. 2017
Friendster 2002 Friendster was a site that allowed users to create their own page to connect with friends and family via instant messenger. In 2009 it was acquired by a Malasyian payment provider, MOL Global. At that time it had about 115 million active users. 2010
Live Journal 1999 LiveJournal was an online blogging platform which hosted blogs on various topics. One of the most popular was a blog called "Oh No They Didn't" which focused on celebrity gossip.

In 2009 the platform was sold to a Russian media company which ceased its US operations. Because of this, it was now subject to Russian jurisdiction and no longer could live under the US' policy of freedom of speech.

LiveJournal is actually still online today but is mostly popular in Russia.

 Not dead!
MySpace 2003 Similar to Friendster, MySpace offered young adults and teens to build their own page and socialize with friends online. Additionally, there was a music sharing element. Many musical artists started sharing their content on Myspace which allowed them to be picked up by agents. Two examples are Lilly Allen and My Chemical Romance. Not dead!
StumbleUpon 2001 StumbleUpon was a "serendipity engine" which allowed for customized and random content to be viewed by the user. StumbleUpon started to go downhill when a major redesign was made to the site in 2012. This caused a major drop off in usage which resulted in layoffs for employees. Also, Garret Camp was the CEO and co-founder. He went on to assist with the creation of Uber. 2018
